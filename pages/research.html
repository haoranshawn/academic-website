Research Philosophy

During my early research, I observed how a poorly designed electronic health record system led to clinician frustration and patient care delays. This experience ignited my passion for understanding how technology can enhance, rather than hinder, human workflows and decision- making. From the beginning of my academic journey, I have been fascinated by how technology mediates human interactions, enhances decision-making, and transforms organizational processes. My research is driven by an interdisciplinary perspective, recognizing that technology's true value emerges when it is seamlessly integrated into human workflows and enhances overall well-being. Specializing in human factors and ergonomic design, I focus on understanding the complex relationships between information systems, human behavior, and organizational effectiveness. My work lies at the intersection of healthcare IT, data analytics, and human-computer interaction, with an overarching goal of optimizing technology's role in supporting both individual and organizational performance.

At the core of my research philosophy is the belief that technology should enhance human capabilities rather than create barriers. My research uniquely bridges the gap between human- computer interaction and healthcare IT by examining how users perceive and actualize technological affordances in high-stakes environments. This work not only advances theoretical understanding but also provides actionable insights for designing more intuitive and effective health technologies. My approach integrates theory development with empirical validation, ensuring that my findings contribute to both academic knowledge and real-world applications. I am particularly interested in how digital technologies afford possibilities for action, how users perceive and actualize these affordances, and how these interactions influence broader organizational and societal outcomes.

A significant portion of my research has been dedicated to healthcare IT affordances, particularly how different user groups such as patients, providers, and administrators engage with digital health technologies. By leveraging the affordance lens, I have explored the complex ways in which mobile health applications, electronic health records, and social media-enabled health systems shape clinical workflows and patient experiences. My research employs a mixed- methods approach, combining qualitative insights from user interviews and observations with quantitative analyses of system usage data. This dual focus allows me to uncover both the behavioral and cognitive dimensions of human-technology interactions. For example, one of my recent projects investigates how patient absorptive capacity and mobile health system innovativeness influence different types of affordances. This study provides actionable insights for healthcare organizations seeking to enhance patient engagement and maximize the value of their digital investments.

My research has been published in information systems and healthcare journals, and I have presented my work at numerous conferences. While I have consistently aimed for high-quality journals, after numerous rounds of reviews and experiencing multiple rejections, I recognize the need to modify my strategy to increase the number of my publications without compromising quality. These studies examine the interplay between user cognition, system design, and organizational outcomes. In another stream of research, I have conducted systematic reviews of healthcare IT affordances to synthesize existing knowledge and identify gaps in theory and methodology. These reviews have played a crucial role in shaping the research agenda for healthcare IT affordances by providing a structured framework that guides future empirical studies. My work has also contributed to methodological advancements, particularly in the assessment of formative measure validations in information systems research, helping to refine the rigor and reliability of quantitative research in the field.

Looking ahead, my research agenda will continue to explore the evolving role of technology in healthcare and beyond, with a particular emphasis on ergonomics and human factors. One key area of interest is the integration of artificial intelligence and machine learning into health information systems. I plan to investigate how algorithmic transparency, system usability, and user trust influence the adoption and effectiveness of AI-driven healthcare solutions. I am particularly interested in exploring how these technologies can be leveraged to reduce health disparities, ensuring that underserved populations benefit from digital innovations. Another promising direction is the study of digital transformation in organizations, particularly how businesses and public institutions adapt to new technologies and navigate challenges related to data privacy, security, and ethical considerations. By examining these issues through the lens of affordances and human-centered design, I aim to provide insights that inform both policy and practice, ensuring that technology is designed and implemented in ways that maximize its benefits while minimizing risks.

My collaborations with experts in cognitive psychology and public health have enabled me to explore the intersection of user cognition and system design, leading to innovative approaches for improving healthcare IT usability. These interdisciplinary partnerships have enriched my research and allowed me to address complex challenges from multiple perspectives. I am committed to expanding my research in ergonomics and human-computer interaction, seeking partnerships that leverage expertise in workplace design, human performance, and cognitive ergonomics. By securing external funding and engaging with practitioners, I aim to conduct impactful research that bridges academia and industry, with a focus on optimizing technology for human well-being and efficiency.

Throughout my academic career, I have remained committed to conducting research that is both intellectually rigorous and socially impactful. My work is driven by curiosity and a passion for understanding how technology shapes human experiences. I take pride in mentoring students and collaborating with colleagues across disciplines, fostering an environment where research thrives through shared knowledge and diverse perspectives. As I continue my research journey, I seek to deepen my contributions to the MIS and ergonomics fields by developing theoretical frameworks that capture the complexity of human-technology interactions while also offering practical solutions for real-world challenges. My goal is not only to advance scholarship but also to create research that empowers individuals, improves organizational effectiveness, and ultimately enhances societal well-being.
